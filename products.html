There is no master catalog on this site, and there never will be. Over the years we've tried nearly everything that comes to market, kept very little of it, and earned the right to represent the lines that remain. A handful of manufacturers in lighting, shading, audio, video, and control.

The pages below describe how we think about each category, the questions we ask before specifying, and what "right" looks like when the install is finished. If a product you've seen isn't listed, ask. Often we've looked at it, sometimes we've fixed it, and occasionally we quietly recommend against it.

The short list

Six categories, curated for the home.

Plate § 01 Light

Fixtures drawn to the room.

A lighting plan is an architectural plan, not a shopping list. We draw it against the floor plans: where light falls, where it doesn't, which circuits dim together, and which finishes match the trim you've already committed to.

We look for
Drivers that don't buzz, dimmers that hold their curve, and keypads we can engrave to the household.
We avoid
Colour-changing novelty, glowing logos, and anything that defaults to an app instead of a wall.
On finish
Five metal finishes, paintable faceplates. Nothing plastic where a hand will land.
Plate § 02 Shade

Shading worked into the wall.

The shades should disappear when lifted and look like fabric, not hardware, when lowered. We design the pocket with the architect, so the headrail lives inside the ceiling detail rather than below it, and we program the run against your sun angles.

We look for
Quiet motors, dual-roller stacks (day-sheer, night-blackout), and fabrics that pass the hand test.
We avoid
Exposed mounts, battery-only systems in high-window rooms, and dangling cords of any kind.
On control
Schedule-and-scene first. Voice and app are nice to have, not nice to need.
Plate § 03 Sound

Loudspeakers you can't point to.

The ceiling grille should read as the smoke detector's quieter cousin, and the invisible series should read as drywall. Each room gets tuned with a calibration microphone so the back of the kitchen doesn't boom and the dining room doesn't hush.

We look for
Flush bezels, paintable trim rings, and amplifiers that size the zones honestly.
We avoid
Cone speakers parked in bookshelves, Bluetooth-only rooms, and subwoofers anywhere a dinner guest can see one.
On outdoors
Landscape satellites on a zoned bus, routed with the irrigation, tuned for the yard's geometry.
Plate § 04 Screen

Displays set flat to the wall.

A television out of its box is a department store. Recessed into the rock and matted flush, it reads as art until it's wanted. We handle the rough-in box, the media closet, and the ISF calibration that comes after the contractor has left.

We look for
Anti-glare panels, art-mode matting, and cable paths that don't involve a visible raceway.
We avoid
"Smart" TVs as the source of record, black-mirror bezels in white-wall rooms, and displays that can't be woken by keypad.
On projection
Reserved for dedicated rooms with light control. The rest of the house belongs to direct-view.
Plate § 05 Control

One app and many fewer switches.

The point of control isn't more control. It's fewer things to remember. Eight switches become one keypad. A scene called "Dinner" handles the dining pendants, the pool lights, and the outdoor speakers in a single press. A houseguest can use it without being briefed.

We look for
Local processors, keypad engraving, and apps that load in under two seconds on bad hotel Wi-Fi.
We avoid
Cloud-only setups, systems that orphan when the vendor pivots, and anything requiring a PhD in scenes.
On routines
We build five. You live with them for a month. Then we come back and adjust.
Plate § 06 Rooms

Spaces built for one purpose.

A dedicated theater, a simulator bay, a media lounge. When a room has one job, we treat it like a studio, isolation, acoustic panels dressed as wall treatments, riser seating measured to the sightline, and a source rack that no one has to see.

We look for
Fabrics that absorb without reading as studio, seating that fits your body, and racks in ventilated closets.
We avoid
Retrofit rooms sold as dedicated, recliner-pit seating where a banquette is better, and simulators that weren't sized for the ceiling.
On delivery
Two calibration visits after punch: one at move-in, one at ninety days.
